Document 01
Circa: Precision Irrigation Platform
Hardware + Software · Hackathon Project · Mar 2026
What it is: Circa is a turret-based smart irrigation system combining ESP32 mesh networking, MQTT, FastAPI, XGBoost soil moisture forecasting, and a multilingual AI chatbot. It targets the inefficiency of rectangular field irrigation using center-pivot principles.
Circa was built in under 48 hours at YHack 2026, where it won 1st Place Overall and 1st Place Societal Impact. I led hardware fabrication, CAD, electronics assembly, and full-stack software integration. It represents the kind of work I care about most: hardware and software working together to solve a problem with real-world stakes. The project demonstrates systems-level thinking, embedded programming under time pressure, and the ability to take a concept from idea to working physical prototype.

Document 02
DocSimplified: AI Form Assistant
Full-Stack Web App · Hackathon Project · Nov 2025
What it is: DocSimplified is a multilingual AI platform that simplifies government forms for low-literacy and ESL users. It includes a browser-based fillable PDF editor, LLM-based step-by-step explanations, text-to-speech generation, and Supabase-backed user profiles.
Built at Technica 2025, DocSimplified won the Intuit Most Transformative Hack award for Gen AI/ML. I worked with teammates to design and ship a tool targeting a real accessibility gap: the millions of people who struggle to navigate complex government paperwork. The project shows my interest in using technology to lower barriers, my ability to integrate multiple APIs and services rapidly, and my growth as a full-stack developer with a product-oriented mindset.

Reflective Statement

The thread running through everything I've done is a bias toward building. Not planning to build, not researching what to build — actually making something, getting it in front of people, and learning from what happens. Whether it's a robot, a drone, a web app, or an ESP32 mesh network mounted on an irrigation turret, the moment I care about most is when something that didn't exist before starts working.

The projects in this portfolio show different sides of that same instinct. Circa started as a question: why does agricultural technology stop at the edge of the rectangular field? It ended as a working prototype, built from scratch in 48 hours, that won two awards at YHack 2026. DocSimplified started from noticing how difficult government forms are for people without legal literacy or strong English skills, and turned into an accessible AI tool that won at Technica 2025. My GitHub is where that pattern becomes most visible — project after project, across robotics, AR, embedded systems, and full-stack web, each one a version of the same impulse to close the gap between an idea and a working thing.

My interest in computer science and mathematics isn't just academic. I study systems because I want to understand how things actually work at a low level, which makes me a better builder at a high level. My time in FIRST Robotics gave me that discipline early — writing autonomous path planning code in Java under competition deadlines, fundraising $50,000+ to keep the team operational, and mentoring younger students taught me that technical skill and leadership aren't separate. They're the same job.

As a candidate, what I offer is adaptability across the hardware-software boundary, a track record of shipping under pressure, and a genuine interest in problems that have human stakes. I want to keep building in environments where that matters. The Gemstone Honors Program and my work on Team Cyborg are pulling me toward research at the intersection of human-robot interaction and assistive technology. My hackathon projects and XR work are pulling me toward applied engineering. I'm comfortable holding both directions at once, and I think the best work usually happens at exactly that kind of intersection.

This portfolio is a partial picture. The resume shows titles and dates. The projects show outputs. What they don't fully capture is what it felt like to debug an ESP32 WiFi instability at 3am, or to refactor a camera pipeline during a live demo, or to figure out how to explain a complex robotics system to a sponsor who had no engineering background. Those moments are where the real learning happened. I'm still early in my career, but I know what kind of work I want to do, and I know I'm capable of doing it.
